01 — Who We Are

The Brand Story

Someone had a slightly unhinged idea: what if a puzzle was a story? Not a pretty landscape you finish and forget. A story with a twist ending that makes the whole table lose their minds.

We make 1000-piece jigsaw puzzles for anyone who's allergic to boring things.

Every Odd Pieces puzzle hides a secret: the box cover is the before. The puzzle you build is the after — same scene, 15 seconds later, completely unhinged. Plus a sealed Secret Ending Envelope you only open when it's done.

The reveal is the whole point. Always has been.

We're not a faceless puzzle company. We're a small, obsessive team who genuinely believe a cardboard box can make an entire room put their phones down.

Grab a drink (or don't), lose four hours, and feel zero regret about it. That's the mission.

Our puzzles exist in a connected universe. Characters cross over. Easter eggs reference other titles. There's a whole metanarrative hiding in plain sight.

Most people don't catch it. The ones who do? They go absolutely feral.

"The gift that surprises everyone — even the person who has everything."

Story-first puzzles built for anyone brave enough to open the envelope.

Reference Brands Areaware's aesthetic sensibility (design-store premium, indie cool, slightly offbeat) + Exploding Kittens' wit (irreverent, genius, fun). NOT Magic Puzzle Company. NOT a generic puzzle brand.
02 — How It Works

The 3-Step Experience

Three acts. One box. Zero preparation for what happens at the end. This is the thing that makes people buy it, gift it, and text about it at 1am.

Step Inside the Story

The box cover is a dense Wimmelbilder illustration — Where's Waldo-level chaos, micro-stories everywhere you look. A before comic inside tells you what's about to happen. You think you know what's going on. You don't.

Build the Aftermath

The puzzle you assemble is the same scene — exactly 15 seconds later. Hundreds of things have changed. You discover them one piece at a time. The moment someone shouts "WAIT — WHAT?!" across the table is inevitable.

Open the Envelope. Lose Your Mind.

There's a sealed SECRET ENDING ENVELOPE inside. DO NOT OPEN IT EARLY. Finish the puzzle first. Then open it. Inside: the after comic that explains everything — plus a final riddle. This is the moment that breaks people in the best way.

You'll find things in here you weren't supposed to find.

04 — Who It's For

Our Audience

Anyone who wants to be surprised. Teens who notice every hidden detail. Adults who need a reason to look up from their phones. Grandparents who've been waiting their whole lives for a puzzle worth doing. If you can handle a WAIT — WHAT?! moment, you're in.

The Curious One

Doesn't matter the age — this person is allergic to boring. They'll spend four hours on something just to find the thing they weren't supposed to find.

Probably the one who spots the Easter eggs first. Definitely the one who texts the group chat about it at midnight.

The Table

Families who need something to actually do together. Couples who want a reason to put their phones in a drawer. Friend groups who discovered that puzzles are secretly the best hang.

Solo puzzlers who put on a playlist and disappear for four hours. All welcome. All correct.

The Gift-Giver

Looking for the thing that makes someone say "How did you even find this?"

Not another candle. Not another generic game. Something that works for the teenager, the parent, and the grandparent sitting at the same table — because good stories work for everyone.

If you can handle a plot twist, you're exactly who this is for.

The Energy That Drives Everything

All Ages.
Zero Chill.

Odd Pieces is for everyone who hasn't decided that surprise is something you age out of.

The teenager obsessed with details. The adult who needs to look up from their phone. The grandparent who's been waiting for a puzzle actually worth doing.

We write for the curiosity — not the age bracket. Our brand voice should work across the whole table.

Shocking before it's cute
Fun before it's refined

✗ Never Sound Like This

"For the whole family!" (too Walmart, too safe)
"An immersive puzzle experience" (dry corporate, immediately skip)
"The perfect gift this holiday season" (zzz)
"Hours of entertainment" (box copy from 1992)
"Premium quality guaranteed" (says literally nothing)
"Recommended for ages 18+" (we don't age-gate — everyone's welcome)
Luxury editorial tone (too stiff, wrong vibe)
Corporate / dry / formal (if a PR department wrote it, delete it)

Voice Principles

Shock First, Explain Later

Lead with the surprising, weird, funny thing. Let curiosity do the work. Don't front-load context.

Confident, Not Arrogant

We know we're different. We don't need to yell about it. Confidence is quiet. Arrogance is loud.

Play It Straight

The best humor is delivered deadpan. Don't wink too hard. Say the absurd thing like it's obvious.

09 — Typography

Type System

Two fonts. No exceptions. Nunito owns the personality. Inter handles the rest. Together they're punchy, clean, and unmistakably Odd Pieces.

Nunito · Google Fonts · Display / Headlines / UI Labels

Nunito 900

Headline 800

Subheader 700

Section Tag / Label · 11px · 800 · +0.18em tracking

Inter · Google Fonts · Body / Functional Copy

Inter Regular 400 — body copy, descriptions, longer reads. This is where the voice of the brand lives in paragraph form. Keep it warm, direct, and never boring.

Inter Medium 500 — callouts, emphasized body, UI text.

Inter SemiBold 600 — small labels, metadata, button text.

Type Rules — Non-Negotiable

Nunito Stays Short

Use Nunito for headlines, labels, and short brand moments only. Never for paragraph-length copy.

Display Effect Is Rare

Stroke + hard shadow is reserved for hero headlines and major brand moments only. Never on body copy.

Inter Does The Reading

Long-form copy, descriptions, and instructions stay in Inter. Keep it clear, warm, and easy to scan.

Display Treatment

Odd Pieces

White text + 2-3px black stroke + 4px 4px 0 black hard shadow.

Rule: Used for hero headlines and key brand moments. Never on body copy.

10 — Art Direction

Illustration Direction

Each puzzle has its own illustration style. That's intentional. What's consistent isn't the style — it's the philosophy. Maximum density. Hidden stories. Comedic payoffs. Wimmelbilder soul.

Maximum Density

Every inch of the illustration is packed with micro-stories. A character in the bottom-left corner has their own subplot. Find it if you can. Wimmelbilder — Where's Waldo-level layering.

Comedic Payoffs

Humor lives in the details. The thing you spot after three hours of puzzling. The absurdist twist you weren't expecting. Never mean-spirited. Always landing.

Pop Culture References

Layered, witty, earned. Not forced. Think: something that makes one person at the table gasp and the others feel like they missed a joke they'd love once explained.

Before to After

Every illustration must work as a before scene AND imply the chaos that follows. The box cover is the before. The puzzle is the after. They must be legible as a pair.

Style Varies Per Title

Pixel art for one puzzle. Clean vector isometric for another. Painterly for the next. The brand doesn't lock one illustration style — each title can look distinct while sharing the Odd Pieces philosophy.

Metauniverse Threads

Every illustration must hide at least one Easter egg referencing another Odd Pieces title. A character, an object, a logo, a detail. The universe is connected. Show it.

On AI vs. Human Artists Odd Pieces works with real artists from around the world. Not AI-generated art. This is brand gospel — the human authorship behind each illustration is part of the product's soul and a key differentiator in how we talk about the work.

Humor Tone for Illustrations

Absurdist ✓

Situations that escalate beyond reason. Vacuum cleaner destroys a city. Mushrooms go full chaos. The premise is weird and the execution commits.

Irreverent ✓

Characters that seem to know they're in a puzzle. Winking references. Meta moments that reward the attentive.

Never Mean ✗

Humor punches up or punches sideways. Never down. The comedy is about situations, not mocking people.

11 — Product

Packaging

Opening the box is the first WAIT — WHAT?! moment. Before a single piece hits the table. It should feel like an Apple box had a baby with a comic book — premium, tight, satisfying, and slightly ominous.

BOX

The Box

Premium cardboard. Tight lid. That slow, satisfying resistance when you open it — like the box knows something you don't. The cover is the before scene: dense, full-bleed, chaotic in the best way. No dead space. Every inch is hiding something.

PRE

Before Comic

Opens the story. Sets the scene. Tells you exactly what's going on — so that when you build the after and realize everything has gone completely sideways, the payoff is even more unhinged. Context makes the twist land harder.

1K

1000 Puzzle Pieces

Premium cut. The image you build is the same scene — 15 seconds after the box cover. Hundreds of things changed. You'll discover them piece by piece. Some will be obvious. Some will make someone at the table yell.

END

Secret Ending Envelope

Do not open early. This is not a suggestion. Sealed inside. Only opened after the puzzle is complete. Contains the after comic — the shocking "why" behind everything — plus a final riddle. The grand reveal. The whole reason you did all of this. Sacred.

WARN

Warning Insert Card

The first brand moment you touch. "This puzzle contains things you weren't supposed to find." It's not just a card — it's a prop from the universe. Sets the tone. Starts the hunt. Should feel like a note slipped under your door by someone who knows too much.

STKR

Sticker Sheet

The surprise at the bottom of the box. In-world characters. Collectible-feeling. The kind of stickers that actually end up on things. Not filler — a genuine delight that people don't expect and definitely keep.

Box Design Principles

  • → Full-bleed illustration — no white borders, no empty branding frames
  • → Title in Nunito 900, uppercase, integrated INTO the illustration — not floating above it
  • → Odd Pieces emblem: present, not dominant. The art is the hero.
  • → "1000 PIECES" callout — confident, visible, no asterisks

The Vibe Check

If someone picks up the box and immediately wants to open it, you nailed it. The lid should resist — that slow, satisfying peel. Every insert lands with intention. Nothing rattles. Nothing feels like an afterthought. The box is part of the story before the story starts.

12 — Distribution

Channel Guidance

Same brand energy, different arena. Shopify gets the full unfiltered personality. Amazon plays by its own weird rules. Kickstarter gets a manifesto. Retail wants to stop you mid-aisle. Know the game before you play it.

WEB

Shopify — oddpieces.com

Home turf. Full brand voice, no filter. This is where Odd Pieces gets to be completely itself.
Lead with blue and yellow, big and loud — this is the brand's living room
Hero section: the 3-step experience front and center — before anything else
Product pages: illustration detail shots that reward close, obsessive inspection
Cross-sell via lore: show the metauniverse — it's not just upselling, it's a revelation
The Secret Ending Envelope: tease relentlessly. Never spoil. Ever.
AMZ

Amazon

The algorithm is not your friend. The images are. Make them do the work.
Main image: clean box on white. No clutter. No cleverness. Just the box.
Secondary images: illustration close-ups that make people stop scrolling
7-image sequence: box → detail → unboxing → in-play → the Envelope → after reveal → whole-table shot
Title: puzzle name first, then "1000-Piece Jigsaw Puzzle" — keep it indexable
Bullets: experience first. "You'll gasps at what changes" beats "300gsm premium board" every time.
K

Kickstarter

Not a product page — a manifesto. Write it like you mean it.
Open with the WAIT — WHAT?! energy. Don't ease into it. Hit them immediately.
Backer tiers: name them like universe lore — not "Early Bird Tier 2" (that's inexcusable)
Campaign updates: talk to backers like they're inside the universe, not customers waiting on an order
Show the team. Show the chaos. The human brand isn't a tactic here — it's the whole pitch.
Stretch goals = universe expansion. Every milestone adds a chapter.
B&N

Barnes & Noble — Retail

Mission: make someone pick it up from 3 feet away without knowing why.
Box cover: bold, dense, visually arresting — it competes against an entire shelf of rectangles
Title: large, uppercase, Nunito 900. No elegance. Just raw presence.
Side panel: "NOT YOUR ORDINARY PUZZLES" — hook them before they even flip it over
Back panel: the 3-step experience, scannable and fast. End on the tease — not the reveal.
No price war vibes. Being unmissable is the only strategy that matters here.
13 — What Makes Us Different

The Human Brand

This is the stuff that can't be faked. The things that separate Odd Pieces from every puzzle company that's just printing cardboard. These aren't features — they're personality.

MAIL

They Mail Puzzle Pieces

If a customer emails about a missing piece, someone on the team literally grabs a puzzle piece and mails it to them. Not a form. Not a replacement box. An actual puzzle piece in an actual envelope. This is the brand in action.

CLUE

Clues, Not Answers

When someone emails asking for the answer to the final riddle, they get clues — not the solution. The experience is sacred. Spoiling it would be a betrayal. The team actively protects the mystery, even when customers beg.

ART

Real Artists, Real People

Every illustration is made by a real human artist from somewhere in the world. Not AI-generated. Not templated. The artists are credited, supported, and part of the brand story. When you buy Odd Pieces, you're also buying someone's work.

TEAM

Small, Passionate Team

Not a faceless corporation. A small team that genuinely believes in what they're building. This comes through in everything — the copy, the packaging details, the customer service. The brand has a pulse because the team does.

The Rule Every brand decision should feel like it was made by a human for a human. If it feels automated, templated, or corporate — it's wrong. The human brand is not a marketing strategy. It's who Odd Pieces actually is.
14 — Rules

Do / Don't

The short version of everything above. Print this out. Put it on the wall. Check every design against it.

✓ Do
Use Odd Yellow (#FEE000) and Blue (#C1E3E8) heavily — both are hero colors
Use Nunito 900 uppercase for all headlines and display text
Lead with the twist, the shock, the "WAIT — WHAT?!" moment
Make the document / design itself feel fun and energetic
Let illustration styles vary per title — the philosophy is what's consistent
Credit and celebrate the artists behind each illustration
Keep the Secret Ending sacred — tease, never spoil
Write copy that sounds like a smart, irreverent human
Use the Wimmelbilder philosophy — maximum density, everywhere
Hide metauniverse Easter eggs in every illustration
Make the flat logo system — no stacked layers, no outlines
The small light blue circle at the bottom of the P — always there
✗ Don't
Add drop shadows, gradients, or outlines to typography
Use the stacked-offset multi-copy logo (that's the old version)
Sound like Magic Puzzle Company — differentiate actively
Use luxury editorial tone ("an immersive puzzle experience")
Sound like a children's activity brand (too soft, too safe)
Use AI-generated art for puzzle illustrations
Spoil the Secret Ending in any marketing material
Automate customer service in ways that feel robotic
Let any channel feel generic, corporate, or templated
Use only yellow and ignore blue (both are dominant)
Position as "for kids" OR "adults only" — it's both, always
Create new puzzle titles that don't connect to the metauniverse
